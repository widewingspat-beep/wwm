What are SERPs?


SERPs, short for Search Engine Results Pages, show the results that come up when someone searches online on platforms like Google. These pages have two main types of results: paid search results and free organic search results.

But SERPs have more to offer. They can also include different types of results, such as search ads, shopping ads, featured snippets, knowledge graphs, videos, and image results. Each SERP is unique because it is shaped by individual users, their search intent, and numerous ranking factors. Even when you use the same search engine and search term, you may still get different results.

Why are SERPs important for SEO?


If it weren't for SERPs, we wouldn't have traditional SEO. The main goal of SEO is to get a high ranking on search engine results pages, or SERPs. Ideally, they want to be in the top three positions. Most people click on the top results, while the second SERP gets much fewer clicks. It's important to understand that being high on Google's search results doesn't automatically lead to a lot of free traffic.

Paid search results can push organic listings down, and many commercial searches may not show any organic results on the first page. Also, more and more people are using "zero-click" searches, where they find immediate answers within the SERP. If you want more organic traffic, use keywords with fewer SERP features. This will make it more likely that people will see and click on your result.

A guide to improving your site's ranking in organic search results.


Organic search results are pages that Google has decided are relevant to a user's search.

Google's complex algorithm uses over 200 factors to rank search results. It considers how relevant they are and their quality for a specific keyword. Although the algorithm's key ranking signals are secret, they have been discovered through various SEO experiments over the years.

These signals can be broken down into three categories. The first category includes off-page SEO indicators, such as the number and quality of websites linking to a specific page. The second category includes on-page SEO cues, such as how relevant and in-depth the content is. The third category includes page experience signals, which include factors like page speed and core web vitals.

When a website appears in organic search results, Google usually shows a title link that can be clicked on, a short description of the website, and the website's URL.

You can influence how this display appears by configuring a page's title tag, URL slug, and meta description, but Google doesn't guarantee that it will use these meta tags.

Google may choose the hard-coded meta description about x% of the time and may show the H1 header tag more often than the title tag element.

Beyond the title, description, and URL, other elements may appear in search results. If Google thinks the date a page was published is important, it may include it. Schema is another tool that you can use. It helps you show ratings, FAQs, or product information.

To get a good spot in organic search results, it's important to understand basic SEO, write content that people searching online will find useful, and have other web pages link to yours.

Now, let's look at the good and bad of organic search rankings:

The advantages of organic rankings are that:

  • Organic rankings won't cost you anything.
  • People click on organic results more often.
  • A high ranking can last for a long time.

The disadvantages of organic rankings

  • It can take months of hard work to get your website to show up in organic search results.
  • Ads and SERP features can make listings appear lower on the page.

Want to know how to get a high ranking in SERP features?


Any search result that isn't a sponsored ad or organic listing is called a SERP feature. Google's first page has many of these features, such as featured snippets, "People Also Ask," "Top Stories," and "Knowledge Graph" results.

Over time, the number of SERP features has increased a lot. This has caused organic listings for some search terms to be moved to a lower part of the page. SERP features provide more information, so users don't have to click through to websites. As a result, the number of people who clicked on organic ads dropped from 67% in 2015 to 39% three years later.

However, this decline in overall CTR doesn't mean you should avoid trying to rank in SERP features. Appearing in these features can significantly increase your click-through rate (CTR).

What are some of the features of the SERP?


While not all features provide a direct link to your website, they appear on the page. Here's a summary:

SERP Features: A Complete List


Google shows different features in its search results, and it tests new ones all the time. Let's look at the most common features that appear in the SERPs and explore ways to improve your site to show them.

Featured Snippets:
  • Featured snippets are those short answers or descriptions that appear at the top of the SERP. They are taken from web pages or videos.
  • Types of texts include paragraphs (text), lists (ordered steps or best-of lists), and tables (data displayed as a table).
  • To be featured, you need to rank in the top ten and format your content correctly.
Direct Answer Box
  • It appears at the top of the search results page for questions people ask, offering short and clear answers taken from public sources.
  • Unlike featured snippets, websites cannot rank here, because answer boxes don't link to or credit the source website.
Knowledge Panel
  • You can find it on the right side of desktop SERPs and near the top on mobile. It provides facts about specific entities, such as people, places, or things.
  • Data comes from Google-approved platforms like Wikipedia, Wikimedia, and Crunchbase.
Local Pack
  • This is shown for searches where the user is looking for a local business. It displays three results, including the business's name, address, phone number, reviews, and a link to the business's website.
  • Optimize your Google My Business profile to appear in the local pack.
Image Pack
  • It appears at the top or elsewhere on the SERP, showing small versions of images from Google Images in response to visually oriented searches.
  • Improve the quality of your images to improve their search engine ranking. However, users who click on results go to Google Images, not your website.
Video Results
  • It displays the video results when the user searches for them. 88% of the video results come from YouTube.
  • Put your video on YouTube and use Video Object schema markup for better visibility.
Top Stories
  • It displays recently published articles, live blogs, and videos in a carousel at the top of the SERP.
  • It's hard to be featured unless your website is approved by Google News, and the ranking doesn't last long because people always want new content.
People also ask:
  • The SERP shows the questions that users often ask Google.
  • It offers an easy way for users to find answers without leaving the SERP.
Twitter Cards
  • It shows the latest tweets from a particular Twitter account in the search results, offering a way to explore them interactively.
  • This is true for both brand queries (where the user is searching for a brand name) and other non-brand queries.
Site Links
  • There are two types: internal links to pages on the same website and sitelinks for branded queries.
